Discover

As a Member of Autism Services Kids Club

I am a member of Autism Services Kids Club of NTU since was a freshman, which is a friendly and caring volunteer club for kids on the autism spectrum as well as their families. We assist kids by participating in group activities, learning living skills, providing a social atmosphere to understand facial expression, body language, and everyday social context that comes naturally to most. We hang out and interact like typical teens or friends, hoping to improve their quality of life through the opportunities to build friendships and have abilities to connect with the real world.

In four years, I accompany with lots of autism kids and keep in touch with their parents. As a volunteer, we provide a service like "respite care", let parents or their families a temporary relief and also a relatively friendly and safety environment for autism kids. However, I keep reflecting: How might we help autism and their families improve their life? How might we help them overcome obstacles they faced everyday? As a result, in 2018 spring, I chose my voluteer experience as topic in a UX design class.

 

 

Autism Services Kids Club of NTU

Interview

Understand the problems and difficulties autism and their families encountered

In order to know more difficulties that autism kids and parents faced in daily life, we started with user interviews from members in the club, including 5 kids, 8 parents, 2 teachers and 3 doctors.

Key Finding from Interviews:

1. Autism kids often forget or lose their stuff and parents need to check or remind him/her many times everyday.
2. It is very wasting time that parents teach autism kids to learn living skills again and again, they hope to find more efficient methods to teach their kids.
3. With correct methods or modes, autism kids can significantly learn and improve the abilities than we expect.
4. Though special education in the school can also assist autism kids to learn living skills and social interaction, but there are still a lot of difference and uncertain variables when they face the real world.
5. Autism have big individual differences, if each kid can be taught by the one who understands him/her most will be the best.

 

User Journey Map: Potential Entrypoints

The results from interviews shed light on our design directions. To analyze our research and identify problem space, we drew out a user journey and saw potential entry points.

Key Finding can mainly distinct into two dimensions:


1. Social Interation Difficulties: There is no fixed pattern to social dysfunction because every kid on the autism spectrum is unique with different strengths and needs at different ages, but it’s almost always one of the major identifiers of autism kids.
2. Lack of Living Skills: Learning practical skills can be challenging for kids with autism, but is essential for independence. (For example: shopping, taking transportation, completing daily chores, housecleaning, doing dishes and cooking). Parents are also worry about whether their kids can cope with real world alone if one day they can not take care of kids anymore.

Shed light on our design directions:

At the beginning, we mainly focus on the problems of social interaction, but after discussing with Proffesor. Tang and doctors, we thought the social dysfunctions originate from "Stereotypic Behaviors", which is defined as repetitive body movement invariance that serves no social function. In school, kids with autism engage in stereotypic behaviors that can often interfere with attempts to teach academic skills. This social unavailability makes it hard for kids to engage in skills such as joint attention or social referencing while in the classroom.

It is complex to solve the symptoms of stereotypic behaviors because it will relate to serious medical issue. More, our original ideal is to help autism kids and their parents reduce pain points they faced everyday. So, how to improve the living abilities of autism kids will be main issue that we focus on later.

Persona: Autism Kids and Parents

Accroding to our analyze of journey map, we mainly found that the problem has two sides: autism kids are lack of practical living skills, and their parents need to teach and remind andkids repeatly because they can not learn these skills efficiently. As a result, we define our design would be suitable for two persona: Autism Kids and Their Parents. Designing personas for autism kid, Eddie Wu, and his parents, The Wus, helped us in making informed design decisions as we had the end user always in mind.

Competitor Analysis

We analysis 20 existing education apps for kids, include learning alphabet, math, geometry, draw and music. There are also a few apps design for learning life skills, like go shopping and take a bus. As we want to help parents teach autism kids life skills, we find that the mainly two problems of existing education tools are:
1. Kids can not connect teaching or gaming content with the real world.
2. Autism have huge individual difference, each one should be taught and treated by customized method, make sure kids receive the right support and reach person-centred needs.

Thus, we chose fidelity level and customized level as competitor analysis standard. We want to design an education app that parents can customize teaching content, and through the high-fidelity gaming, kids can learn practical living skill efficiently.

Design Concept

1. Gamification: Through gamification, fun can be put back in the learning equation for kids and increase engagement, also be poised to augment the way kids learn in schools and at home.
2. High-Reality: Each stage display in high-reality, help autism kids connect learning content with real world.
3. Customized-Teaching Content: Parents can customize teaching content for their kids, which is the real needs to solve daily difficulties.

User Testing

 

At first, we designed prototype and conducted usablity tests both for desktop and app. We found that autism kids are more easier to use app rather than desktop because some autistm kids also have muscle weakness when they control the mouse. Thus, we mainly focus on usability test for app and conducted thirty minute long 1:1 sessions with 5 autism kids and 7 parents. After testting, parents did a series of qualitative questions regarding the prototype system’s interface and functionality. The overall impression was positive. Kids played the games with enthusiastic, and parents commented on the features of customizing game content could help them teach their kids efficiently, also reduce feeling of fatigue that teach kids repeatedly.

What I learnt

I am so thankful to have the opportunity to did this project which keep in my mind for a long time. As a designer, I learn a couple of valuable lessons with this project. One is that no one solution can entirely solve a serious problem, only a little part of it. The idea of this project is related back to what I saw during the volunteer service in Autism Services Kids Club of NTU. During my design process, I maintained a close contact with foundation for autistic children and adults in Taiwan, a non-profit organization helping autistic children and adults to deserve a better life. They gave me several feedbacks based on their professional experience and help me iterate the design to fit the need in the real world. I think the valuable part of this project is to provide an opportunity for autism and their parents to make their challenging life easier. By solving this trivial stuff happens in their every day, I hope it can eventually add on to a better experience.
